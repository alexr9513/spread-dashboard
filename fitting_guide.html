Curve Fitting Methods

How the dashboard estimates the spread-to-maturity relationship

The dashboard fits a curve through an issuer's bonds to visualise the term structure of spreads and identify cheap/rich bonds (residuals above or below the curve). You can choose between four methods via the ⚙ panel, or let Auto pick for you.

0 Auto Mode

Auto selects the most appropriate method based on how many bonds the issuer has. It is the default and the recommended setting for most use cases.

BondsMethodWhy
3 – 4 Polynomial (deg 2) Interpolates exactly with 3 points; smooth parabola with 4
5 Nelson-Siegel Enough data for the 4-parameter model; imposes an economic shape
6 + Spline Flexible, handles outliers gracefully, GCV tunes smoothing automatically

1 Linear

simplest Ordinary least squares straight line. One slope, one intercept, no curvature.

$\text{OAS}(t) = a + b \cdot t$

The slope $b$ captures how many basis points of spread you gain per additional year of maturity. Useful as a quick sanity check or when the issuer has very few bonds clustered in a narrow maturity range where curvature is not observable.

When to use
3 bonds all within 1–2 years of each other, or when you explicitly want a no-frills baseline.

Solid line on the chart. No tuneable parameters.

2 Polynomial

flexible degree 2 – 4 Fits a polynomial of the chosen degree via ordinary least squares.

$\text{OAS}(t) = c_0 + c_1 \cdot t + c_2 \cdot t^2 \quad \text{(degree 2)}$

A degree-2 polynomial (parabola) is the sweet spot for small samples: with 3 bonds it interpolates exactly through every point, and with 4–5 bonds it captures mild curvature without overfitting. Higher degrees (3, 4) can capture S-shapes or humps but need more data to avoid wild extrapolation.

ParameterValuesGuidance
Degree 2, 3, or 4 Degree 2 is almost always sufficient. Use 3+ only with 8+ bonds and visible inflection.
Watch out
Polynomials can blow up outside the data range. The curve may dive negative or spike at maturities shorter or longer than your bonds. This is fine for interpolation (bonds that exist) but meaningless for extrapolation.

Dotted line on the chart.

3 Nelson-Siegel

parametric 4 parameters The classic term-structure model, widely used for government yield curves and adapted here for credit spreads.

$\text{OAS}(t) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot \varphi(t, \tau) + \beta_2 \cdot \bigl[\varphi(t, \tau) - e^{-t/\tau}\bigr]$
$\text{where} \quad \varphi(t, \tau) = \dfrac{1 - e^{-t/\tau}}{t/\tau}$

Each parameter has an economic interpretation:

ParameterRoleInterpretation
β₀ Level Long-run spread the curve converges to at very long maturities
β₁ Slope Short-end premium (or discount). Positive = inverted, negative = upward sloping
β₂ Curvature Hump or trough in the belly. Controls the "bulge" around τ years
τ Decay Where the hump peaks (in years). Small τ = fast decay, hump near short end

By default, all four parameters are estimated jointly using Adam gradient descent with multiple restarts. You can also fix τ to a specific value (1y, 2y, 5y, etc.), in which case β₀, β₁, β₂ are solved analytically via OLS — this is faster and more stable when the optimizer struggles (common with few bonds in a narrow maturity range).

SettingValuesGuidance
τ decay Est. (auto), 1y, 1.5y, 2y, 3y, 5y, 7y, 10y "Est." lets the optimizer find τ. Fix it when the curve looks unstable or when bonds span a narrow range.
When to use
5+ bonds with reasonable maturity dispersion. The model's parametric structure prevents overfitting and gives economically interpretable parameters that you can compare across issuers. Preferred when you want to reason about curve shape (flat? inverted? humped?).
Watch out
NS imposes a specific functional form. If the issuer's bonds don't follow a level-slope-curvature pattern (e.g. two distinct clusters from senior and subordinated debt mixed together), the curve may distort to accommodate outliers. Consider filtering by bond type first, or switching to Spline which is more flexible.

Dashed line on the chart. Legend shows β₀, β₁, β₂, τ, and RMSE.

4 Spline

flexible bloomberg-style Penalized cubic B-spline — the approach used by most dealer analytics platforms. Fits a smooth curve through the data without imposing a global parametric shape.

$\hat{c} = \arg\min_c \;\bigl\| \mathbf{B} c - y \bigr\|^2 + \lambda \cdot c^\top \mathbf{P}\, c$

The first term is the goodness of fit (how closely the curve passes through your bonds). The second term is the roughness penalty — it penalizes wiggly curves. The balance between fit and smoothness is controlled by λ.

The key components:

ComponentWhat it doesDetail
B Basis matrix Cubic B-spline basis functions evaluated at each bond's maturity. Each basis function is a "bump" centered around a knot.
c Coefficients Weights for each basis function. The fitted curve is a weighted sum of bumps.
P Penalty matrix Second-difference matrix. Penalizes rapid changes in successive coefficients, which corresponds to penalizing the curve's second derivative (curvature).
λ Smoothing Trade-off: λ = 0 → interpolation (passes through every point). λ → ∞ → straight line. Selected automatically by GCV or set manually.

Interior knots are placed at quantiles of the bond maturities, so they adapt to where the data actually sits (denser knots where there are more bonds). The number of knots controls the curve's maximum flexibility — more knots = more degrees of freedom.

SettingValuesGuidance
Smoothing λ Auto (GCV), 0.001 – 1000 Auto (GCV) uses Generalized Cross-Validation to pick the λ that minimizes leave-one-out prediction error. This is the recommended default.
Low λ (0.001–0.1): curve closely follows every point — good for clean data, bad with outliers.
High λ (100–1000): heavily smoothed, almost linear — good when you suspect noise or outliers.
Interior knots Auto, 2 – 8 Auto uses n_bonds / 3, capped at 8. More knots = more local flexibility. With 6–8 bonds, 2–3 knots is plenty. With 15+ bonds, 4–6 knots lets the curve capture local features. Rarely need more than 6.
When to use
6+ bonds, especially when the issuer has enough data to reveal local curve features that NS would smooth away (e.g. a kink around the 5y point, or a flattening at the long end). Also the best choice when there are outlier bonds — GCV will increase λ to smooth through the consensus, and the outliers show up as large residuals (cheap/rich signal).
Watch out
With fewer than 6 bonds, the spline falls back to linear (not enough data for a meaningful fit). Unlike NS, spline parameters (coefficients) have no direct economic interpretation — you can't say "the long-run spread level is X" by reading a single number. Extrapolation outside the data range is unreliable.

Long-dash line on the chart. Legend shows knot count (k), λ, and RMSE.

Residuals & the Value Signal

Regardless of which method you use, the residual for each bond is:

$r_i = \text{OAS}_i - \widehat{\text{OAS}}(t_i)$

A positive residual means the bond trades wider than the curve (potentially cheap). A negative residual means it trades tighter (potentially rich).

The shaded band around the curve represents ±1 RMSE. Bonds outside this band are the strongest cheap/rich candidates. In the tooltip, the residual is shown in basis points when you hover over a bond in FIT CURVE mode.

Important
A large residual doesn't always mean mispricing — it can reflect genuine differences in bond features (callability, coupon, seniority, liquidity) that the curve doesn't capture. Always check the bond's characteristics before acting on a residual signal.

RMSE

The Root Mean Square Error measures overall fit quality:

$\text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} r_i^2}$

Lower RMSE = tighter fit. Typical values for EUR HY issuers:

RMSE (bps)Interpretation
0 – 10ExcellentBonds sit tightly on the curve
10 – 30GoodNormal dispersion for HY
30 – 80ModerateSome outlier bonds or mixed seniority
80+PoorConsider filtering by type or changing method
